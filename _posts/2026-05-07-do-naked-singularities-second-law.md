Black-hole thermodynamics begins with an entropy puzzle. When ordinary matter crosses an event horizon, its microphysical information and entropy appear inaccessible to the external universe. The second law is preserved by assigning entropy to the black hole itself, proportional to horizon area, and by treating horizons as thermodynamic objects.

This raises a sharp conceptual contrast with naked singularity spacetimes. If no event horizon forms, then matter and information are not permanently hidden behind a causal boundary in the same way. In that classical picture, entropy may remain externally connected through radiation, scattering, or other strongly nonlinear processes near the singular region.

The key idea is not that naked singularities are already thermodynamically solved. It is that the black-hole information tension is tightly linked to horizons. With no horizon, the structure of the paradox may change significantly.

At the same time, quantum behavior near naked singularities is still open. We do not yet have a complete analogue of Hawking's framework for generic horizonless singular collapse. Whether information is recoverable in practice, and how unitarity is realized, remain active research questions.

This leads to a broader question: could cosmic censorship also carry a thermodynamic interpretation, beyond purely geometric predictability? If gravitational collapse outcomes are constrained by entropy consistency, then observations of compact objects may test not only geometry but also deep thermodynamic principles.

In this perspective, black holes and naked singularities are not just alternative endpoints of collapse. They may represent different ways spacetime organizes information.
