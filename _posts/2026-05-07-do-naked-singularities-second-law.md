If horizons rescue the Second Law for black holes, do naked singularities avoid the paradox entirely?

Black hole thermodynamics did not begin with a complicated calculation. It began with discomfort. Imagine taking an ordinary object — say, a coffee mug — and dropping it into a black hole. The mug is mundane by any standard. It has temperature, internal energy, and microscopic degrees of freedom. In thermodynamic language, it carries entropy.

Now consider what happens when it crosses the event horizon.

From the perspective of an external observer, the mug’s entropy is no longer accessible. It has not merely been scrambled or diluted — it has disappeared behind a causal boundary. But the Second Law of Thermodynamics insists that the total entropy of the universe should never decrease. If entropy can vanish irretrievably into a black hole, one of the most reliable principles in physics appears threatened.

Jacob Bekenstein took this paradox seriously and proposed a radical idea: black holes themselves must possess entropy. Even more remarkably, this entropy is proportional not to volume, but to the area of the event horizon. With this insight, a geometric quantity acquired thermodynamic meaning.

When matter falls into a black hole, the accounting works out neatly:

- The black hole gains mass
- The horizon radius increases
- The horizon area increases
- Black hole entropy increases

The Second Law survives — though only after gravity compels a profound reinterpretation of entropy itself.

Stephen Hawking’s discovery that black holes radiate made this thermodynamic description unavoidable. Black holes are not merely gravitational objects; they behave as thermodynamic systems.

### Now remove the horizon.

Suppose gravitational collapse were to produce a naked singularity instead, which is the case in the gravitational collapse of many physically relevant matter clouds. No event horizon or delayed event horizon. No permanently hidden region. No absolute information barrier. In such a spacetime, the entropy carried by infalling matter might not vanish irreversibly from the observable universe.

Whatever violent interactions occur near the singularity could still produce radiation, scattering, or highly scrambled — yet in principle observable — signatures.

The entropy outside need not decrease fundamentally because nothing enforces permanent concealment.

This contrast is subtle but conceptually provocative. Black holes compel us to introduce horizon entropy, Hawking temperature, and a generalized version of the Second Law. Naked singularities, at least in a naive classical picture, do not obviously impose the same necessity. Entropy might remain in circulation rather than being sequestered behind a causal boundary.

### An important caveat is unavoidable.

Hawking radiation relies on the horizon structure, and the quantum behavior of naked singularities remains poorly understood. It is not yet clear whether they radiate generically, whether information is genuinely recoverable, or how quantum gravity would modify the story. Unlike black hole thermodynamics — which rests on firm mathematical ground — the thermodynamic status of naked singularities remains largely unexplored.

However, the deepest trouble arises precisely because of the event horizon. Hawking’s semiclassical calculation shows that black holes radiate thermally, and as they slowly evaporate, this radiation appears to carry no specific information about the matter that fell behind the horizon.

The horizon enforces a causal separation: one member of an entangled pair escapes as radiation while its partner remains inside, leading to the well-known black hole information paradox — an apparent violation of quantum unitarity if the black hole completely evaporates. In this sense, the very existence of an event horizon turns what would otherwise be a gravitational collapse into a profound challenge for the foundations of physics.

A naked singularity, by contrast, offers a possible way out. Without an event horizon to permanently hide information, the infalling matter and its information could, in principle, remain connected to the outside universe. The highly energetic and chaotic processes near the singularity might convert the collapsing matter into observable radiation, or into highly scrambled but, in principle, recoverable signals, allowing information to remain in circulation rather than being irretrievably lost.

In this way, naked singularities appear conceptually less problematic for the laws of quantum mechanics.

Still, the comparison invites a deeper speculation.

Roger Penrose’s cosmic censorship conjecture proposes that singularities formed in realistic gravitational collapse are always hidden by horizons. The traditional motivations emphasize predictability. Yet thermodynamics quietly hovers nearby. Black holes preserve the Second Law only after entropy is attributed to horizons. Naked singularities might, at least conceptually, avoid the paradox differently — by never fully hiding entropy in the first place.

Could cosmic censorship, then, possess a thermodynamic interpretation?

Perhaps the allowed outcomes of gravitational collapse are constrained not only by geometry and dynamics, but also by the requirement that entropy accounting remain consistent without ambiguity.

This possibility opens intriguing directions for thought and research. One may ask whether thermodynamic laws can be formulated in spacetimes without horizons, whether quantum fields near naked singularities exhibit particle creation analogous to Hawking radiation, and whether observable signatures — high-energy emission, accretion dynamics, gravitational waves — might reveal differences in entropy behavior near horizonless singularities.

Black holes forced physics to merge gravity, thermodynamics, and quantum theory. Naked singularities — if Nature permits them — may challenge that union from an entirely different angle.

And perhaps the most fascinating implication is that spacetime geometry may not be the only judge of collapse —entropy may also have a vote.
